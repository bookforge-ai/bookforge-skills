# Architect Involvement Recommendation

## Context

You're a new architect joining a team of 20 senior developers who have been working together for 3 years on a complex distributed system, with 2 more years to go.

## My Recommendation

This is a nuanced situation. On one hand, the team is highly experienced and well-established, which suggests they don't need heavy-handed oversight. On the other hand, you're new to the team and the system is complex.

### Start by Listening

For the first few weeks, I'd recommend a **listening-first approach**:
- Attend their existing meetings as an observer
- Understand their current architecture and the decisions that led to it
- Build relationships with key team members
- Don't make any sweeping changes right away

### Calibrate Your Involvement

Given that most are senior engineers with 3 years of working together:

- **Don't micromanage.** These developers know the system better than you do right now. Trust their technical judgment on implementation details.
- **Focus on the big picture.** Your value-add is cross-cutting concerns, long-term architectural vision, and strategic decisions.
- **Be available, not overbearing.** Make it clear you're there to help, not to gatekeep.
- **Own cross-team coordination.** With 20 developers, coordination across different parts of the system is where you can add the most value.

### Specific Level of Involvement

I'd say aim for about **30-40% involvement** in day-to-day technical decisions:
- Attend weekly architecture syncs
- Review major design decisions that affect multiple services
- Let individual developers or sub-teams handle their own implementation choices
- Step in only when decisions have cross-cutting architectural impact

### Watch Out For

- Being seen as an outsider imposing changes on an established team
- Trying to prove your value by making changes too quickly
- Ignoring the existing culture and norms the team has built

### Longer Term

Over time, as you build trust and understanding, you can adjust your involvement level. If you see areas where the architecture needs improvement, propose changes collaboratively rather than dictating them.

The key insight is: **your authority comes from trust, not from title.** Earn it first.
